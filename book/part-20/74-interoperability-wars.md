# 74. Interoperability Wars

## Lead

The central strategic contest will be interface ownership. Telcos, banks, fintechs, state systems, and digital-asset operators each want to be the user's primary dashboard while relying on each other's infrastructure.

The rail is interoperability governance. Builders include standards groups, platform alliances, and Application Programming Interface (API) governance teams. Users include businesses and citizens moving across services. Referees include regulators and competition authorities.

Behavior will favor platforms that reduce switching friction and support portable identity and payment logic.

Shadow: walled-garden incentives can slow national productivity.

## Context

Kenya built multiple strong rails in parallel: telco-led payments, bank channels, fintech overlays, and government platforms.[src-04][src-10][src-66] The next strategic contest is how these rails connect without forcing users to repeatedly restart identity checks, payment setup, and trust verification.

Interoperability matters because fragmentation now carries real economic cost: failed transfers, duplicate integrations, and delayed service delivery.

## History

- **2007-2020:** Dominant rails scale through closed and semi-open ecosystems, with APIs expanding access unevenly.[src-04][src-05][src-66]
- **2021-2025:** Open-banking and modernization agendas gain visibility, pushing for safer and more portable integration patterns.[src-76]
- **2026 onward:** Interoperability moves from technical preference to policy and competition issue.

## Product and mechanics

Interoperability depends on shared standards across identity, payments, messaging, and dispute handling. Core mechanics include API contracts, common error taxonomies, reconciliation formats, and service-level expectations between institutions.

For users, successful interoperability feels like continuity: fewer dead ends when moving between apps, channels, and institutions.

## Business model and incentives

Closed ecosystems maximize capture; interoperable ecosystems maximize total market expansion. Providers must decide whether to optimize for short-term control or long-term transaction growth.

The usual model becomes mixed: open core rails for broad reach, differentiated premium layers for margins.

## Regulation and referees

Referees include financial regulators, communications regulators, competition authorities, and consumer-protection frameworks that discourage anti-competitive lock-in while preserving safety.[src-16][src-18][src-76]

Regulatory clarity on access, pricing transparency, and liability during failed handoffs is essential for trust in interoperable systems.

## Adoption in Kenya

Adoption begins where switching costs are highest: merchant payments, bill collection, payroll, and government service payments. SMEs adopt interoperability tools when they reduce reconciliation pain and improve cashflow predictability.

Larger institutions tend to pilot bilateral integrations first, then migrate toward more standardized multi-party interfaces.

## Ecosystem effects

Better interoperability lowers entry barriers for new builders and reduces dependence on single gateway providers. It unlocks aggregator businesses, integration-audit services, and portability-focused user products.

At system level, it strengthens resilience by reducing single points of failure.

## Setbacks and controversies

The recurring controversy is "open in principle, closed in practice." Some ecosystems publish APIs but maintain opaque access terms, uneven quality, or punitive economics for smaller players. Another risk is interoperability without security discipline, which can expand attack surfaces if controls are inconsistent.

Policy fights are inevitable when incumbents defend profitable choke points.

## Competition and alternatives

The alternatives are vertical super-app control, bilateral partnership webs, or standardized shared rails. Kenya is likely to run all three in parallel, with outcomes determined by execution quality and referee consistency.

The long-term winners may be firms that combine high interoperability with superior reliability and user trust.

## Legacy and open questions

Open questions remain around pricing fairness, liability allocation across multi-party failures, and how quickly standards can evolve without breaking backward compatibility. Interoperability wars are ultimately governance wars disguised as API debates.

## Builder read

Build for portability from day one. Use explicit contracts, publish quality metrics, and define failure ownership before scale. A major whitespace is neutral "trust infrastructure" that verifies counterpart reliability across fragmented ecosystems.
