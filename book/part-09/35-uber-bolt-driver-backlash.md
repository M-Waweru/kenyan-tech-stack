# 35. Uber and Bolt: Growth Meets Driver Backlash

## Lead

**August 2016**, **Nairobi**: drivers signed up with **Uber** march to the company’s offices after a fare cut they read as an existential threat. **Simon Mutembei** tells **Reuters** they want the old prices back—“I need to eat. I need to feed my family”—and names **Little Cab** as a live alternative ([Reuters — Kenya Uber strike][reuters-uber]). The wire’s numbers are blunt: **Uber** had begun in **January 2015**; it was cutting total fares by up to **35%** to stimulate demand amid “growing competition from local firms”; price per kilometre had just fallen to **Kenyan shillings (KSh) 35** from **KSh 60**, with the waiting rate per minute cut too; drivers wanted take rates dropped from **25%** to **15%** of earnings ([Reuters — Kenya Uber strike][reuters-uber]).

The **rail** is two-sided marketplace **dispatch**—**global positioning system (GPS)** maps, dynamic pricing, ratings, in-app payments—mapped onto **Kenyan** fuel economics, **traffic**, and **labour** politics. **Builders** are **product** managers, **data science** teams, local operations, and trust-and-safety staff. **Users** are riders chasing predictable fares and drivers chasing net income after **fuel** and **hire-purchase** **vehicle** costs. **Referees** are **National Transport and Safety Authority (NTSA)**-adjacent processes, **county** transport politics, driver associations, and the court of public opinion when screenshots of earnings go viral.

**TechCrunch** reporting from **June 2016** captures how aggressively **Uber** “**Africa**-nised” the model: **cash** payments across multiple countries, with **Nairobi** described as only the second city globally where **cash** was tested; **Alon Lits** is quoted tying **Kenya**’s **cash** rollout to a claimed three-fold usage increase, alongside **M-PESA** and **Paga** partnerships ([TechCrunch — Uber Africa cash][tc-uber-cash]). The same piece records **Bitange Ndemo** describing street-level friction—physical fights and attacks on drivers—while noting **Uber**’s price undercut forced legacy operators toward **GPS** and better pickup discipline ([TechCrunch — Uber Africa cash][tc-uber-cash]).

By **July 2019**, **The Star** framed a different referee beat: a **July 2018** memorandum of understanding (**MoU**) between platforms—including **Uber**, **Bolt** (then still widely known as **Taxify**), and **Little**—and drivers, meant to adjust commissions and per-kilometre customer charges; drivers struck, alleging the **MoU** sat unimplemented, while **NTSA** was named as the ministry’s instrument to manage the file ([The Star — Uber Bolt strike, MoU][star-mou]).

The tension is **growth metrics versus driver solvency**. What riders call a discount, drivers call a wage cut—unless trip volume compensates, which **fuel** spikes can erase overnight.

## Context

**Uber** landed after **smartphone** and **mobile money** culture had already rewired **Nairobi** ([Part II](../part-02/index.md), [Part III](../part-03/index.md)). **M-PESA** did not stop **cash** from mattering in ride-hail; it layered alongside it ([TechCrunch — Uber Africa cash][tc-uber-cash]).

## History

### 2015: Nairobi uberX

**Uber**’s **Kenya** blog announced **Nairobi** **uberX** as a structured alternative to street haggling—use this as **operator** colour, triangulated with press ([Uber — Nairobi uberX][uber-nairobi]).

### 2016: Cash, competition, and the fare war

**Cash** optionality and **OkHi**-style addressing experiments signalled that **Silicon Valley** defaults (card-only, perfect street addresses) would not survive contact with **East African** urban form ([TechCrunch — Uber Africa cash][tc-uber-cash]).

**Little Cab**’s entry and **Uber**’s responsive cuts turned pricing into a public negotiation; **Reuters** documented drivers threatening platform switching and riders stranded or price-gouged by non-app taxis during the strike ([Reuters — Kenya Uber strike][reuters-uber]).

### 2018–2019: MoU politics and Bolt naming

**The Star**’s strike reporting is the cleanest short snapshot of institutional escalation: **NTSA** mandate, **county** directives, and driver claims that per-kilometre rates had collapsed from **Sh60** at introduction to **Sh16** by **2019**—treat as contested advocacy, not audited statistics ([The Star — Uber Bolt strike, MoU][star-mou]).

## Product and mechanics

### Composite scene: Two phones, three apps

*Composite, grounded in multi-homing behaviour documented across **Reuters**/**TechCrunch**/**Star** reporting:* A driver mounts two phones—one on **Uber**, one on **Bolt**—and watches surge tiles while **WhatsApp** driver groups forward rumoured bonus codes. The “product” is not any single **user interface**; it is portfolio management across commissions, quests, and fuel burn.

Mechanically, platforms must reconcile **cash** trips, **mobile money**, incentives, fraud, and identity checks—each a **failure** mode when **connectivity** drops mid-ride.

## Business model and incentives

Take rates (**25%** in **Reuters**’ **2016** frame for **Uber**) fund growth, insurance layers, and support desks; drivers experience them as a structural wage bill ([Reuters — Kenya Uber strike][reuters-uber]). **Dynamic** pricing shifts **consumer surplus** in minutes; **driver** associations try to slow that clock through strikes and **MoU** politics ([The Star — Uber Bolt strike, MoU][star-mou]).

## Regulation and referees

**NTSA** and ministry steering appear in **The Star** as the formal shell around voluntary **MoU** governance—implementation gaps become political fuel ([The Star — Uber Bolt strike, MoU][star-mou]). **Personal** data—trip history, home drops, payment tokens—falls under the **Data Protection Act**, **2019** ([Data Protection Act][dpa]) and **Office of the Data Protection Commissioner (ODPC)** practice ([Office of the Data Protection Commissioner][odpc]).

## Adoption in Kenya

Press coverage consistently emphasises **Nairobi**-centric early markets—“services have not spread beyond the capital” was **Reuters**’ **2016** read—so adoption geography stayed uneven even as brand recognition went national ([Reuters — Kenya Uber strike][reuters-uber]).

## Ecosystem effects

**Uber** normalised mapped hail, star ratings, and **digital** receipts—habits **Little**, **Bolt**, and later delivery apps inherited. It also proved that **global** **playbooks** must be recompiled for **cash**, **security**, and **addressing** ([TechCrunch — Uber Africa cash][tc-uber-cash]).

## Setbacks and controversies

Strikes, violence against drivers, and earnings strikes on social media are recurrent. **MoU** non-implementation narratives show limits of self-regulation without enforceable referee decisions ([The Star — Uber Bolt strike, MoU][star-mou]; [TechCrunch — Uber Africa cash][tc-uber-cash]).

## Competition and alternatives

[36. Little Cab](36-little-cab.md), **Bolt**, street taxis, **matatu** culture, and employer shuttles compete for the same minutes in **traffic**.

## Legacy and open questions

Legacy: **Kenya** became a teaching case in **localising** **marketplace** economics. Open questions: **electric**-vehicle **total cost of ownership**, **platform** **worker** classification law, and whether **county** governments will standardise **per-km** floors.

## Builder read

*Interpretation.* If you move people for money, model **fuel** **shocks** before **promo** codes. Publish **commission** and **per-km** history like a **changelog**. Assume drivers will **multi-home**—your “loyalty” is a spreadsheet cell.
