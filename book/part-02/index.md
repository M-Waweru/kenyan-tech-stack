# Part 2: Connectivity (2005–2012)

Part II is where Kenyan digital ambition moved from rhetoric to infrastructure. Policy bets, subsea capacity, platform gravity, and what happens inside households when the router stays on are one continuous story: throughput is useless until it becomes habit, and habit is fragile until maintenance, retail, and regulation agree on what “working” means.

## Topics in this part

- [4. Bitange Ndemo's Bet](04-bitange-ndemo-bet.md)
- [5. The Subsea Cable Shock](05-subsea-cable-shock.md)
- [6. Safaricom: Platform Incumbent Is Born](06-safaricom-platform-incumbent.md)
- [7. Home Internet and Wi-Fi Culture](07-home-internet-wifi-culture.md)

The chapters run in order: Dr. Bitange Ndemo’s policy phase names the national stack (international ingress plus inland backbone); the subsea wave changes wholesale economics without erasing last-mile physics; Safaricom shows how distribution and payments ritual turn a telco into default infrastructure; home fibre and Wi-Fi show how “always on” rewires schoolwork, hustles, and support loads—usually with mobile tethering still in the back pocket.

## Deep-Dive Analysis

Read this part as infrastructure history, not startup chronology. The interpretive method is the same as Part I: after each rail appears, watch how users transact with scarcity, how builders translate capacity into service, and how regulators turn arguments into measurable outcomes. The chapters below are written to keep those pressures visible in ordinary rooms—county offices, landing-station handoffs, agent kiosks, living-room routers—not only in policy PDFs.

## Why this part matters for builders

Connectivity is not backdrop in Kenya. It is an input variable for unit economics, support load, and trust. Teams that internalize Part II ship better fallbacks, calmer incident comms, and GTM that assumes dual-rail households—not ideal fibre alone.
