# 5. The Subsea Cable Shock

## Lead

At landing stations, speeches celebrate history. In network operations rooms, people watch utilization charts.

Kenya’s subsea moment was not symbolic. It changed the cost-and-capacity equation that had constrained everything from software outsourcing to media streaming and enterprise cloud use.

The rail here is international bandwidth plus the domestic systems required to distribute it. The builder is consortia, carriers, backbone operators, and internet service providers. The referee is regulatory structure and market behavior. The user is everyone who now expects the internet to be always there.

Bridge: once scarcity relaxed, advantage shifted to whoever could own customer trust, distribution, and service integration.

## Context

Before subsea plurality, East African connectivity was expensive and brittle. Demand was rising, but many use cases were uneconomic at scale.

CCK’s FY2010/11 report marks the break: Kenya had access to **TEAMS**, **East African Submarine Cable System (EASSy)**, and **SEACOM**, and the report explicitly links this to higher speeds, greater availability, and reduced broadband costs ([CCK annual report FY2010/11][cack-2011]).

## History

The key sequence:

- TEAMS is presented in Kenya’s Vision 2030 reporting as a strategic affordability intervention and became operational in the 2009/10 window ([Vision 2030 progress report 2008–2017][vision-2008-2017]).
- EASSy is publicly announced by the **International Telecommunication Union (ITU)** in 2010 as a major regional addition ([ITU release][itu-eassy]).
- SEACOM provides another major route into East and Southern Africa ([SEACOM official][seacom]).

Then adoption numbers accelerate. In FY2011/12, total internet subscriptions rose to **7,738,882**, up from **4,258,287**, while estimated users reached **14,032,366** ([CCK annual report FY2011/12][cack-2012]).

## Product and mechanics

### Worked example (*Composite*) — why landing a cable does not instantly fix a user complaint

A startup in Nairobi migrates to cloud video workflows after hearing “new cable capacity is available.” Upload speed improves at office hours but fails unpredictably in evenings. The root cause is not subsea scarcity; it is local congestion and last-mile contention. This is exactly why the chapter separates international capacity from distribution mechanics. Kenya’s modern lit international capacity figure (22,154.438 Gbps in Q3 FY2024/25) can rise even while specific local user experience remains volatile ([CA sector statistics Q3 FY2024/25][ca-q3-2025]).


Subsea cables solve only one part of connectivity.

User experience depends on the full chain: lit international capacity, inland transport, metro distribution, and last-mile access quality. That is why national capacity upgrades can coexist with neighborhood frustration.

The same logic appears in modern CA statistics. By Q3 FY2024/25, Kenya reports **22,154.438 Gbps** total lit international bandwidth capacity, with named undersea systems including **SEACOM, TEAMS, EASSY, LION 2, DARE 1, and PEACE** ([CA sector statistics Q3 FY2024/25][ca-q3-2025]).

## Business model and incentives

Subsea economics are wholesale-first and capex-heavy. Operators then choose how gains are allocated across pricing, margin recovery, and reinvestment.

As routes diversify, bargaining leverage improves. But retail affordability still depends on competition intensity and distribution economics.

## Regulation and referees

Regulation matters at every layer: landing rights, interconnection, competition, and consumer quality safeguards.

The infrastructure lesson from the first subsea wave remains true in later data: resilience is multi-layered. You can have abundant international capacity and still fail users through local bottlenecks.

## Adoption in Kenya

CCK’s FY2011/12 table shows where growth landed first: **terrestrial mobile data/internet subscriptions** reached **7,655,576**, while **fixed fibre subscriptions** rose to **49,371** from **22,460** the previous year ([CCK annual report FY2011/12][cack-2012]).

That mix captures a Kenyan pattern that still holds: mobile drives scale, fixed drives high-intensity use in covered zones.

## Ecosystem effects

Subsea abundance changed how builders planned:

- cloud and software workflows became less fragile,
- media products became richer,
- and regional collaboration became more practical.

This chapter is where Kenya moves from internet scarcity management to internet growth management.

## Setbacks and controversies

The biggest misconception was speed of translation. Landing a cable is fast. Spreading its benefits fairly is slow.

Pass-through to households is shaped by competitive dynamics, geography, and local infrastructure reliability. The political narrative often moved faster than those realities.

## Competition and alternatives

Multiple cables reduced single-route risk and improved wholesale optionality. But they did not eliminate concentration questions in downstream consumer and platform markets.

Once international scarcity eased, competition turned toward user ownership and ecosystem control.

## Legacy and open questions

The subsea era reset Kenya’s baseline. “Can we get enough international capacity?” stopped being the central question.

Today’s harder questions are affordability distribution, middle-mile hardening, and whether high-capacity access can spread beyond core urban corridors at the pace demand now requires.

## Builder read

*Interpretation.* Treat network assumptions as product assumptions.

In Kenya, performance and trust are won in volatility: outages, congestion spikes, and uneven last-mile conditions. Build resilience patterns into the core experience.
