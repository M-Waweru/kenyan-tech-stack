# 54. Stablecoins: The Parallel Dollar

## Lead

When a currency feels slippery, a “digital dollar” that lives on a phone can look like a practical hedge rather than a speculative bet. Stablecoins reframed crypto for many users: not “number go up,” but “hold value, settle fast, avoid bank friction.”

The rail is dollar-denominated settlement and storage through virtual-asset channels: wallets, exchanges, peer-to-peer desks, and on-chain transfers. Builders include wallet providers, exchanges, OTC operators, compliance tooling teams, and liquidity market makers. Users include freelancers, traders, importers, and households seeking value preservation or faster international settlement. Referees include financial regulators and legal frameworks still adapting.

The behavior shift is that more users treat virtual assets as utility rails: receive cross-border income, store value in a dollar proxy, and move funds without traditional correspondent banking. The shadow is custody risk, fraud exposure, and policy uncertainty: users gain flexibility while absorbing technical and legal risks.

## Context

Stablecoins sit downstream of remittances and upstream of regulation. They compete with bank FX, remittance apps, and informal corridors, while relying on a parallel infrastructure stack (exchanges, wallets, and P2P liquidity). Chainalysis’ Sub-Saharan Africa analysis describes crypto in the region as retail-driven and notes a shift toward stablecoins as users seek less volatility than Bitcoin, often as an inflation mitigation and trading vehicle ([Chainalysis — Sub-Saharan Africa adoption][chainalysis-ssa]).

## History

### From Bitcoin-first narratives to stablecoin utility

Early crypto adoption leaned heavily Bitcoin narratives. Over time, volatility and repeated market shocks made stablecoin rails more attractive for day-to-day settlement and savings.

### Kenya: “parallel dollar” meets payments culture

Kenya’s payments culture makes the mental model easy: paybill, confirm, repeat. Stablecoins plug into that same desire for instant confirmation—just on a different settlement network.

## Product and mechanics

Users acquire stablecoins via centralized exchanges, P2P marketplaces, or OTC desks. Funds are held in custodial accounts or self-custody wallets, then transferred on-chain. Off-ramps convert back to local currency via P2P or exchange withdrawals.

## Business model and incentives

Exchanges monetize via trading fees and spread; P2P platforms monetize via ads, fees, or embedded services. OTC desks monetize via spread and speed. Stablecoin issuers monetize via reserve yield and distribution partnerships.

## Regulation and referees

Kenya’s formal perimeter for virtual asset service providers is set by the **VASP Act, 2025** on Kenya Law ([VASP Act, 2025][vasp]). Prior to such frameworks, central banks globally—and CBK locally—warned about consumer protection, AML, and platform failure risks; CBK’s 2015 virtual currencies circular is a canonical primary reference for that early posture ([CBK Circular 14 of 2015][cbk-circ]).

## Adoption in Kenya

Stablecoin use concentrates where users have cross-border links: diaspora households, remote workers paid by international clients, and traders/importers facing dollar needs. Adoption also follows information channels: WhatsApp and Telegram groups that share “rates,” “escrow,” and “trusted sellers.”

## Ecosystem effects

Stablecoins create a parallel pricing layer: informal FX rates, wallet-to-wallet transfers, and new middlemen who look like agents but operate digitally. They also raise pressure for formal rails to improve speed, cost, and transparency.

## Setbacks and controversies

Scams, fake exchanges, and loss of funds from poor custody practices remain major harms. Policy uncertainty can also cause sudden de-risking by banks and payment providers, pushing activity further into P2P shadows.

## Competition and alternatives

Alternatives include bank dollar accounts, money transfer apps, and informal corridors. Stablecoins win where bank friction and costs are high and where counterparties already accept crypto settlement.

## Legacy and open questions

Will stablecoins become a mainstream cross-border settlement layer in Kenya, or remain a niche rail for digitally fluent users? Regulation, banking integration, and consumer protection will decide whether the “parallel dollar” becomes a bridge or a wall.

## Builder read

*Interpretation.* If you build stablecoin rails for Kenya, design for trust primitives: identity, dispute handling, and clear rate disclosure. The technical transfer is easy; the hard part is safeguarding users from counterparties and operational collapse.
