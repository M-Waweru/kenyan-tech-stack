# Part 1: Before the Hype (1968–2007)

Part I is the hidden guild: the rails Kenya had before the app store and before **M-PESA** became the country’s default mental model for “digital.” Core banking batches, cybercafés as the first mass interface, and **Global System for Mobile Communications (GSM)** scale trained integrators, intermediaries, and muscle memory that later money and platforms would inherit—not erase.

## Topics in this part

- [1. Banking in the Dark](01-banking-in-the-dark.md)
- [2. The Cybercafe Republic](02-cybercafe-republic.md)
- [3. Telcoms Before the Boom](03-telcoms-before-the-boom.md)

The chapters run in order: banking’s night-shift discipline and vendor culture; assisted access at the cybercafé counter; then prepaid **SIM** retail, **short message service (SMS)** habits, and duopoly competition that set the runway for mobile money (**M-PESA** went commercial **6 March 2007**). Read them as one stack—settlement, human help, pocket reach—not three unrelated industries.

## Deep-Dive Analysis

Read this part as infrastructure history, not startup chronology. After each rail appears, track how users learn to trust machines and intermediaries—PINs and balances at the ATM, the attendant who fixes the upload, the **SIM** card as identity before **know your customer (KYC)** regimes catch up. The chapters keep pressure visible in ordinary rooms: reconciliation queues, café price boards, airtime kiosks—not only in annual reports.

## Why this part matters for builders

Regulated batch discipline, shared-PC threat models, and **unstructured supplementary service data (USSD)** muscle memory are not “legacy”—they are why Kenyan products can assume certain user skills and certain failure modes. Teams that skip this layer misread integration culture, agent economics, and why “confirmation SMS” lands as proof.
