# 63. GiveDirectly: Cash as Climate and Shock Infrastructure

## Lead

When drought, illness, or income collapse hits, unrestricted cash can restore agency faster than scripted aid bundles. Instead of prescribing priorities, cash lets households decide what stabilizes life first.

The rail is direct digital transfer for resilience. Builders include transfer operators, payment partners, and verification systems. Users include vulnerable households navigating overlapping shocks. Referees include evaluators, donors, and public accountability frameworks.

The behavior shift is broader acceptance of cash-first intervention logic where evidence supports outcomes. The shadow is targeting tension: who gets included, who is left out, and how communities perceive fairness.

## Context

Kenya’s digital payments infrastructure made large-scale transfer execution viable. The policy debate moved from “can cash be delivered safely?” to “when is cash superior to in-kind aid?”

## History

### Experimental evidence era

NBER work on cash transfers in Kenya documented general-equilibrium and welfare effects beyond recipient households, pushing cash from NGO experiment to serious policy instrument ([NBER — general equilibrium effects of cash transfers][nber-cash]).

### Long-horizon basic income experiments

GiveDirectly’s Kenya basic-income work expanded attention from emergency aid to multi-year income security experiments ([GiveDirectly — basic income program][gd-ubi]).

## Product and mechanics

Typical mechanics include eligibility mapping, mobile money enrollment, transfer cadence design (lump-sum vs periodic), fraud controls, and monitoring/evaluation loops.

## Business model and incentives

For transfer operators, credibility depends on leakage minimization, targeting clarity, and measurable impact. Donors increasingly demand transparent data on both recipient outcomes and non-recipient spillovers.

## Regulation and referees

Cash transfer programs intersect AML checks, data-protection obligations, and public-sector social-protection coordination where programs run alongside state systems.

## Adoption in Kenya

Adoption is strongest where households trust payout rails and where transfer timing matches seasonal or emergency needs.

## Ecosystem effects

Cash rails influence local markets, debt repayment, school retention, and household investment behavior. They also pressure traditional aid models to justify higher overhead and lower agency.

## Setbacks and controversies

Targeting disputes, social frictions, and fraud attempts remain persistent operational risks. Program legitimacy depends on transparent criteria and grievance handling.

## Competition and alternatives

Alternatives include in-kind aid, voucher systems, and subsidized services. Cash tends to outperform where household priorities are diverse and markets function.

## Legacy and open questions

Will cash transfer rails become default shock infrastructure in climate-vulnerable settings, or remain donor-contingent projects? Durability depends on financing models and state-program integration.

## Builder read

*Interpretation.* If you build resilience-transfer systems, design for legitimacy as much as logistics: publish targeting logic, grievance windows, and audit trails. Trust decays faster than transfer throughput if fairness is unclear.
