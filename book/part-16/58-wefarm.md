# 58. WeFarm: SMS Knowledge as Infrastructure

## Lead

A farmer sends a pest-control question on a basic phone and gets practical replies from peers. No smartphone, no broadband, no extension officer on site—just a message rail that turns distributed experience into usable advice.

The rail is low-bandwidth farmer knowledge exchange. Builders include agritech messaging platforms, routing algorithms, and community-curation workflows. Users include smallholders with limited formal extension access. Referees include agricultural policy systems, quality trust in peer answers, and platform governance.

The behavior shift is that agronomic advice becomes faster, social, and locally contextual. The shadow is information variance: bad advice can travel as quickly as good advice, and sustainability is hard when support and monetization models change.

## Context

Kenya’s agricultural extension gaps and uneven internet access made SMS-first architecture a rational design choice. WeFarm’s model sits between state extension and private input sales, using farmer-to-farmer answering as a distributed support layer.

## History

### SMS peer network at scale

Reuters profiled WeFarm as a text-message network connecting offline farmers in Kenya, emphasizing low-bandwidth accessibility as the core innovation ([Reuters — WeFarm SMS network][reuters-wefarm]).

### Venture phase and product expansion

TechCrunch later reported WeFarm’s funding and user growth (about 2.5 million users at the time), showing investor belief in farmer-network effects and adjacent services ([TechCrunch — WeFarm USD 11M][tc-wefarm]).

## Product and mechanics

Farmers submit questions via SMS, platform systems route to relevant peers, and responses return as message threads. Value depends on language coverage, routing quality, moderation, and turnaround speed.

## Business model and incentives

Potential revenue layers include marketplace commissions, input partnerships, data products, and bundled services. Incentive risk appears when monetization pressures push product priorities away from answer quality.

## Regulation and referees

Because WeFarm-like rails can influence farming decisions and input purchases, they intersect with consumer protection and agricultural policy objectives even without being regulated like lenders or banks.

## Adoption in Kenya

Adoption favors users with feature phones and low data budgets. Trust accumulates through repeated useful answers, not branding alone.

## Ecosystem effects

SMS knowledge rails reduce information latency and can improve bargaining and planning confidence. They also create pathways into bundled rails—credit, input fulfillment, and insurance.

## Setbacks and controversies

Main risks are advice quality inconsistency, weak attribution of outcomes, and platform sustainability under funding cycles.

## Competition and alternatives

Alternatives include state extension agents, agro-dealer advice, radio programs, WhatsApp groups, and direct input-company outreach.

## Legacy and open questions

Can low-bandwidth farmer knowledge rails remain trusted public-interest infrastructure while evolving viable business models? The answer depends on governance of advice quality.

## Builder read

*Interpretation.* If you build farmer-information rails, optimize for relevance and accountability over raw message volume. A smaller network with reliable agronomic signal beats a noisy one with fast churn.
